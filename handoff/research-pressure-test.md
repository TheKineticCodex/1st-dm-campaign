# The Second Lantern: Research Report & Pressure-Test of PROMPTV2.md

## TL;DR
- **Build the immersion features — most are genuinely novel, and the stack is sound — but reorder around three hard risks: Supabase free-tier project pausing (fatal for a game played weeks apart), pollinations.ai's zero-moderation player-facing portraits, and iOS PWA storage eviction.** The directive is well-conceived; the changes are about sequencing and guardrails, not direction.
- **What's novel vs. derivative:** the finger-drawn fey-contract signature and live cross-device carnival minigames appear to have NO prior art; sealed whispers and per-character audio are only ad-hoc precedented; the iPad table mode and live dice feed are well-precedented (D&D Beyond Shared Dice, Arkenforge, Group RPG Dice Roller do them already) — so they should be treated as table stakes, not differentiators.
- **Legal exposure is low but non-zero:** SRD 5.2 (CC-BY-4.0) covers your mechanics safely with a one-line attribution; a private, free, unlisted fan app referencing Witchlight/Prismeer/Feywild is squarely inside WotC's Fan Content Policy; the real risk vector is the AI portrait feature, not the D&D rules content.

## Key Findings

1. **The in-person-augmentation niche is largely open, but not empty.** Named products already deliver iPad "table mode" (Arkenforge, Tableward, TaleSpire) and live dice feeds (D&D Beyond Shared Dice, Group RPG Dice Roller). No shipped product bundles secret whispers + minigames + contracts + audio into an immersion-first in-person companion. The carnival minigame sync and finger-drawn fey-contract signature appear genuinely novel.
2. **The "phones at the table" debate is real and cuts against you unless you design carefully.** The dominant community view is that phones *break* in-person immersion (distraction, screens over faces). Your app must justify every screen-glance as additive to the fiction, or it inherits the same objection.
3. **The technical stack is validated but has three sharp edges:** Supabase pauses Free-plan projects "that show low activity over a 7-day period" (with a 90-day window to restore before deletion); iOS PWAs enforce a 7-day cap on script-writable storage and cap cache at ~50MB; and pollinations.ai offers no SLA and no reliable moderation.
4. **Witchlight is a strong fit for this app's philosophy** (rules-light, roleplay-first, milestone-based) but has known pacing problems in Chapter 1 (the carnival sandbox) and needs deliberate handling for a 3-player party.
5. **SRD 5.2 is safe and permanent; the adventure content is covered by fan policy** as long as the app stays free, private, and unlisted.

## Details

### Area 1 — Prior Art / Competitive Landscape

**The big VTTs are the wrong comparison.** D&D Beyond, Foundry VTT, Roll20, Owlbear Rodeo, Alchemy RPG, Demiplane, Shard Tabletop, and Dungeon Alchemist are overwhelmingly built for *virtual* tables or as *reference/character-sheet* tools. Owlbear Rodeo is "the digital equivalent of a dry-erase battle mat" — maps and tokens, no rules, no whispers. Alchemy RPG is the one that leans "narrative-first" with animated backgrounds, music, and sound effects, and is now free, but it is still fundamentally a VTT for remote/streamed play. None of these is your competitor; they solve a different problem.

**The real prior art is a small, specific set of in-person tools:**
- **Arkenforge (The Master's Toolkit)** — explicitly markets itself for in-person play as "an interactive DM or GM screen," with TV/projector table mode, fog of war, dynamic lighting, and mini-detection. It is priced around US$35 standalone / US$250 for the Ultimate Bundle. Its focus is maps/audio/lighting — **not** whispers, minigames, or contracts.
- **Tableward** — the closest philosophical competitor: a "live companion tool for tabletop RPGs" with a TV/tablet table-mode second screen (initiative, party sheet, villain portrait, current map) while the DM panel stays private. Free during beta, paid tier planned. No whispers, minigames, contracts, or audio features. Cloud-only.
- **TaleSpire** — a 3D VTT ($25/person) used in-person via a TV, with real-time synced dice on the board, but reviewers note "there is no player view popout so Talespire is decent but not great for in person play" and it requires being online to load maps.
- **Live dice feeds are a solved problem.** D&D Beyond "Shared Dice" (free, now on mobile) lets the whole party see 3D dice rolls "even when your character sheet is in the palm of your hand," explicitly for tables "gathered around the table." The free "Group RPG Dice Roller" (by Alexis Panagides) does exactly the player-phones-stream-to-a-shared-table pattern with turn notifications and DM-only HP tracking. **Your NEXT-1 dice feed is therefore derivative — treat it as table stakes, not a differentiator.**
- **Secret whispers to phones are done ad-hoc, never as a polished in-person product.** DMs repurpose Discord DMs/bots; there is an open Discord feature request for a `/whisper` "secret message" command. Critically, the D&D Beyond community both *wants* this and *fears* it: one DM notes "physical notes are preferable to texts since the content is much less likely to be noticed by the other players. Texts on someone's phone can be very easy to see by accident." Your **sealed whispers** feature addresses a real unmet need — but the "easy to see by accident" risk means the UI must default to a sealed/tap-to-reveal state, not an always-visible message.
- **Per-character audio "memory fragments" are near-novel.** Ambient audio tools exist (Syrinscape, Pocket Bard, Tabletop Audio) but none deliver private per-character audio to individual devices. The closest precedent is **Alice is Missing** ($21.99), a silent RPG played entirely via text messages on players' real phones, where "each card comes with a voicemail prompt, a message each player records for the end of the game." Your Reliquary (owner-uploaded GarageBand melodies revealed per-character) is a fresh variation on this.
- **The fey-contract signature and carnival minigames appear genuinely novel** — no board game, TTRPG, or app was found implementing a finger-drawn in-fiction contract signature or tap-based minigames with live cross-device position sync. The nearest analog to the signature is legacy games where players physically sign/name components (Betrayal Legacy ships "1 sealed box and 1 sealed envelope" and has players sign family members across 14 in-game "years").

**Bottom line for Area 1:** Your differentiators are the *integration* plus the four features nobody ships polished (sealed whispers, carnival minigames, fey contracts, memory fragments). The dice feed and table mode are catch-up features that make the app feel complete but win no ground.

### Area 2 — Licensing and IP

**SRD 5.2 (released April 22, 2025, under CC-BY-4.0; the SRD 5.2.1 errata followed May 1, 2025, adding 15 accidentally-omitted magic items) is your safe harbor for mechanics.** It contains the 2024 core rules content — classes, spells, conditions, weapon mastery, the rules glossary, many monster stat blocks — and is *irrevocable*: "Placing the SRD under a Creative Commons license is a one-way door. There's no going back." You may use it commercially or privately; the only requirement is a short attribution (a couple of sentences), far lighter than the old OGL.

**Know the exclusions.** SRD 5.2 deliberately omits IP-flavored content: the Artificer class, the Aasimar species, the Beholder and other iconic monsters, and named entities (Strahd, Orcus, Tiamat). It even renames the Deck of Many Things to "Mysterious Deck" and the Orb of Dragonkind to "Dragon Orb." If your app references any of these by their protected names as *rules content you reproduce*, you're outside the CC license. Note that SRD 5.1 (also CC) already released some terms (Strahd, Deck of Many Things) irrevocably, so those specific names remain usable via 5.1 if ever needed. For a Witchlight campaign, you'll mostly need SRD-safe basics (conditions, standard spells, standard monsters), which are all present.

**The adventure itself (The Wild Beyond the Witchlight) is NOT in any SRD.** Prismeer, the Hourglass Coven, Mister Witch & Mister Light, Zybilna, the named carnival attractions — these are WotC IP. You cannot reproduce the adventure's text. But you *can* reference and build around them in a **private, free fan app** under WotC's Fan Content Policy, whose core rule is verbatim: "One word: F-R-E-E." The Policy explicitly lists "websites" as covered Fan Content. The constraints that matter for you:
- **Keep it truly free and open-access.** The policy states verbatim: "You can't require payments, surveys, downloads, subscriptions, or email registration to access your Fan Content." A login wall for your 3 players is a gray area; a public paywall or mandatory email signup is not permitted.
- **Include the required notice:** "[Title] is unofficial Fan Content permitted under the Fan Content Policy. Not approved/endorsed by Wizards. Portions of the materials used are property of Wizards of the Coast. ©Wizards of the Coast LLC."
- **Don't use WotC logos or trademarks or the ampersand trade dress.** The Policy says don't use "Wizards' logos and trademarks." Referencing setting *names* in prose (Feywild, Witchlight, Prismeer) is standard fan-content practice; reproducing the D&D logo or the stylized ampersand as your app's branding creates trademark exposure. Use your own wordmark ("The Second Lantern," "The Song the Sea Forgot") and avoid the official logo.
- **Practical reality:** WotC has never pursued a private homebrew tool used by one table, and the community consensus is that they never would ("They will never hunt down a DM who homebrews for their table"). Your actual risk here is close to zero as long as the app stays private/unlisted.

**The AI portrait feature is your real IP and reputational risk, not the D&D content.** pollinations.ai:
- **Offers no warranty and no reliability guarantee.** Its terms state the service is provided without warranty that it "will be uninterrupted, timely, secure, or error-free." Independent 2026 reviews confirm "Pollinations does not offer formal SLA or uptime guarantees."
- **Content ownership/attribution:** You retain ownership of and responsibility for content you post; you're responsible for ensuring you have rights to anything you submit; "similar content may be generated for other users based on similar prompts." Commercial use "depends on the model license and service terms" — it aggregates multiple underlying models (Flux, GPT Image, Seedream, Nanobanana), whose licenses vary, so the safest posture is non-commercial fan use only.
- **Moderation is weak.** The broader class of open image generators is documented to produce NSFW/violent/biased output; one 2024 academic study of the most-downloaded open models found "a complete lack of any refusal behavior or safety measures." For a *player-facing, one-shot* portrait at a character-creation ritual, an inappropriate or grotesque output at an emotional moment is a live risk.

### Area 3 — Technical Validation

**Supabase Realtime is adequate for your scale but you must know the free-tier ceilings (2026):** 200 concurrent peak connections, 2 million messages/month, and a 256KB recommended max message payload (the hard byte-size limit is 1MB). The rate limit is **~20 messages/second on Free (50 on Pro)**. For a 3-player table streaming dice and snail-race positions to one iPad, you are nowhere near the connection or message ceilings — but the **20 msg/sec rate limit is the one to respect** for the live snail race: throttle position broadcasts (e.g., 10Hz max, batched) rather than emitting on every tap. Presence is more expensive than raw broadcast: "every time you send a presence message it will count as a message for each of the clients it goes to plus the send." Prefer Broadcast over Presence for high-frequency updates; reserve Presence for lightweight "who's connected" state.
- **Broadcast reliability note:** Broadcast messages are stored in daily partitions and dropped after 72 hours; they are fire-and-forget, not a durable log. For anything that must survive (dice history, minigame results), write to Postgres, don't rely on the realtime message.
- **A known abuse/cost caveat:** with only an anon key, a malicious actor can spam realtime messages; there's an open, long-standing discussion about this. Mitigation: use **private channels with RLS** and authenticated access, which Supabase now supports.

**Scheduled/delayed message delivery (NEXT-4) — use the right tool for the reliability you need:**
- The standard pattern is `pg_cron` + `pg_net` to invoke an Edge Function on a schedule, or the newer **Supabase Cron** dashboard UI (built on pg_cron) which supports sub-minute schedules (every 1–59 seconds), SQL/function/HTTP/Edge-Function jobs. Store a `deliver_after` timestamp on each message row and have a cron job sweep for due messages.
- **Critical reliability caveat:** "With pg_cron and Supabase Cron, a paused project or an outage silently pauses every schedule… Skipped runs are not retried." For a game played weeks apart on the free tier, this compounds the project-pausing problem below. For truly time-sensitive between-session delivery, an **external cron service hitting an Edge Function** (with retries/alerts) is more robust than in-database cron.
- Auth pattern note: the old static `service_role_key` approach and `pgjwt` are being deprecated (Postgres 17); use Supabase Vault + the current recommended pattern for cron→Edge-Function auth.

**Finger-drawn signatures (NEXT-2):** Use **szimek/signature_pad** — the de-facto standard, HTML5-canvas-based with variable-width Bézier interpolation, works in all modern mobile browsers, no dependencies, exports PNG/JPG/SVG and serializable JSON point data. Two gotchas: (1) you must handle high-DPI/retina scaling with `devicePixelRatio` and re-scale on resize/orientation change (resizing the canvas clears it); (2) set `touch-action: none` on the canvas to prevent scroll-while-signing. It uses the modern Pointer Events API path in current versions.

**Web Audio / uploaded audio playback on iOS Safari (NEXT-3):** iOS Safari is the strictest environment — it "blocks all autoplay with sound, period." You cannot auto-play the memory-fragment melodies. The required pattern is the well-established "tap to start / unlock" gesture: show an explicit button ("Play the melody" / "Unlock audio") and call `play()` (or resume the AudioContext) *inside* the user-gesture handler. Also set `playsinline`. This is actually on-theme — a deliberate tap to "listen to the memory" fits the ceremony.

**PWA install/offline on iOS (2026):** Meaningfully better than it was, but with hard limits you must design around:
- Push notifications work on iOS 16.4+ **only when installed to the Home Screen** (not in a browser tab). As of iOS 26, home-screen sites default to web-app mode.
- **Cache Storage is capped around 50MB per origin** — do not cache the uploaded audio and sigil art aggressively; keep the app shell small.
- **The killer for your use case:** iOS enforces a 7-day cap on script-writable storage, and PWAs added via "Add to Home Screen" can be evicted after ~7 days of disuse, taking cached data with them (home-screen-installed apps persist longer than in-browser storage, but should not be relied on across weeks-long gaps). Since your sessions may be weeks apart, an offline-first design that relies on cached data persisting between sessions will fail on iOS. Treat the local store as a *fast cache that can vanish*, with Supabase (when reachable) as the source of truth — which your dual LocalStore/SupabaseStore pattern already anticipates. **Background Sync is not supported on iOS**; sync on next foreground open using the `online` event.
- No automatic install prompt on iOS — you must teach players to "Add to Home Screen" manually.

**Tailwind v4:** The one real gotcha for an existing app is that v4 is a ground-up rewrite: config moved from `tailwind.config.js` to CSS-first `@theme`, `@tailwind` directives replaced by `@import "tailwindcss"`, the PostCSS plugin split out (use `@tailwindcss/vite` for your Vite setup — it's the faster, recommended path), and some class renames (`bg-gradient-to-*` → `bg-linear-to-*`, `shadow-sm` → `shadow-xs`). Two silent-failure traps: default border color is now `currentColor` (not gray), and **dynamically constructed class names get purged** (`"bg-" + color` won't work — use complete class strings or `@source` safelisting). Targets Safari 16.4+/Chrome 111+/Firefox 128+; fine for your audience. Since your app is already on v4, the main ongoing risk is dynamic class construction in the ceremony/animation code.

**prefers-reduced-motion (for the ceremony-heavy UI):** This is essential given the divination forge, sigil reveals, and animated ceremonies. Motion can trigger vestibular reactions (dizziness, nausea, migraines) in a population estimated in the tens of millions. Best practice per WCAG 2.3.3 and MDN: don't kill all animation — *replace* motion (scale/slide/parallax) with opacity/color fades under `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)`, and provide an in-app toggle too (for shared devices and users who haven't set the OS flag). For JS-driven animation, gate on `window.matchMedia('(prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)')`.

### Area 4 — First-Time DM & Table Design

**Witchlight is an excellent match for a first-time DM and for this app's philosophy.** It's rules-light, roleplay-forward, and uses **milestone advancement** (not XP) — combat is optional and the party can complete it without violence. Characters go from level 1 to ~8. This is forgiving for a new DM and aligns perfectly with an app that emphasizes ceremony, secrets, and story over combat math.

**Known problems and fixes:**
- **Chapter 1 (the Witchlight Carnival) pacing is the classic sticking point.** It's a large sandbox of eight attractions, and "it can be common for groups to want to split up… DMs will want either to try to keep the group together or prepare to jump quickly between groups." The ticket is good for eight attractions. **This is where your app helps most:** the carnival minigames and the shared table display can *pace* the sandbox and give a spotlight structure that keeps a splitting party engaged. Community advice: the Mystery Mine is "a bit brutal for so early."
- **Track the wishes and clues.** NPCs like Kettlesteam (kenku warlock), Diana Cloppington (carousel), and Northwind & Red (who grant wishes) plant clues and payoffs that matter as late as Chapter 4. **A DM's Book that surfaces these callbacks is high-value** — this is a natural fit for your quote wall / callback features (NEXT-4/5).
- **Add merchants.** The module under-provides places to spend gold; the app's DM's Book could hold a homebrew merchant table.

**3-player party balancing:** Witchlight's low-combat design already softens the small-party problem, but for the fights that do happen, the standard levers are **sidekick rules** (from Tasha's/the module — a sidekick NPC that scales with the party) and scaling encounters down. Since advancement is milestone-based, you don't need to worry about XP division. A sidekick also gives you a diegetic vehicle for DM-triggered "tether" effects (NEXT-5).

**Session Zero is the single highest-leverage thing a first-time DM can do.** Consensus across sources: budget 1.5–3 hours; align on tone/lethality; set safety tools (Lines & Veils, X-Card); and — most relevant to your app — **explicitly set the "phones at the table" policy.** Build characters *together* and forge inter-character connections ("I'd recommend every player at the table have some kind of connection with at least one other player"). Your **divination forge** ritual is essentially a structured, gamified Session Zero character-creation tool — lean into that framing. The "six truths" of the setting and a written, shared agreement are recommended.

**Design theory that validates the directive:**
- **Secret information as a mechanic** is a proven investment driver (hidden-traitor games, and legacy games' sealed envelopes: "sealed envelopes only to be opened at certain points… create a more customized and personal experience"). Your sealed whispers and tether effects tap this directly.
- **Personalization and callbacks** increase investment — legacy games' permanent, personalized alteration of components is the mechanism. Your Reliquary (player-uploaded melodies), quote wall, and per-character tethers are well-grounded in this.
- **Ceremony/ritual** in games raises the emotional stakes of ordinary actions (the finger-drawn signature turns "you accept the pact" into a felt, physical commitment — the same reason legacy games have you *sign* components).

**The phones-at-the-table caution (critical for adoption):** The community is genuinely split, and the default skeptical position is that phones *destroy* in-person social play through distraction ("so easy to get distracted by a phone"; "when your phone use slows down play… that's disrespectful to your other players"). The nuance that saves your app: the objection is to *off-task* phone use (social media, texting), not to *on-task, in-fiction* device use. The design imperative is that every interaction be **fast, glanceable, and diegetic** — the phone should feel like a lantern/artifact in the story, not a browser. Long menu-pecking is the failure mode ("scrolling up & down to try and find something"). Favor the iPad table mode as the shared focal point and keep phone interactions to seconds.

### Area 5 — Risk / Gap Analysis Inputs

- **Supabase free-tier project pausing is the #1 operational risk.** Per Supabase's official docs, "Supabase pauses Free Plan projects that show low activity over a 7-day period," and once paused there is a 90-day window to restore the project from Supabase Studio before it can be deleted; "Paid projects cannot be paused." A campaign played every 2–3 weeks will find its backend paused before every session, requiring a manual un-pause (and there are no automatic backups on free, and no point-in-time recovery). This directly threatens the between-session features (NEXT-4), scheduled delivery, and any "check the app during the week" behavior.
- **iOS PWA storage eviction (7-day cap on script-writable storage)** compounds this: local cached data may be gone by next session. Design for cold-start-from-server every session.
- **pollinations.ai downtime + inappropriate output** at a player-facing, one-shot, emotionally-loaded moment (the character-creation ritual). No SLA; weak moderation.
- **Vercel Hobby limits (2026):** per Vercel's pricing page (last updated Feb 27, 2026), the Hobby plan includes "100GB bandwidth, 1 million edge requests, 1 million function invocations, 4 CPU-hours of compute, and 100 build minutes per month," and "when you hit any limit, your project pauses instead of billing you." For a 3-player app this is enormously comfortable. **Two watch-outs:** (1) "the Hobby plan is non-commercial only" — fine for a private fan app, but never monetize it on Hobby; (2) Next.js `<Image>`/image optimization and ISR can silently generate function invocations — but you're on Vite/React SPA, so this is largely N/A.
- **Notable 2025-2026 stack changes:** Supabase is deprecating legacy anon/service_role JWT keys by end of 2026 in favor of publishable (`sb_publishable_…`) / secret (`sb_secret_…`) keys — plan a key migration. Vercel moved to Active CPU pricing (default by early 2026) and now enforces hard Hobby caps with feature-lockout rather than silent overage. Tailwind v4's Oxide engine is stable and works on Vercel with no special config.

## Recommendations

**Do these before the next build phase (blocking):**
1. **Solve Supabase pausing first.** Either (a) upgrade to Pro ($25/mo) — the cleanest fix and it also removes the pausing, adds backups, and raises the realtime rate limit to 50 msg/sec; or (b) if staying free, add a lightweight external uptime pinger (e.g., a scheduled GitHub Action or cron-monitor hitting a health endpoint) to keep the project warm, AND set up the free GitHub-Actions-to-R2 backup pattern since free has no backups. **Benchmark to upgrade:** the moment between-session features (NEXT-4) ship, go Pro — delayed delivery is worthless if the scheduler silently pauses.
2. **Add a safety net to the AI portrait feature.** Make it opt-in, add a visible "regenerate" and "skip/choose from a curated fallback set" control, pre-moderate with a simple prompt allowlist, and have a graceful offline fallback (a set of hand-picked default sigils/portraits shipped with the app) for when pollinations.ai is down. Never let a single AI output be unskippable at the ceremony. Add the CC/model-license note and keep the feature non-commercial.
3. **Design the local store as a disposable cache, not a source of truth.** Given iOS eviction + Supabase pausing, every session should be able to cold-start from the server, and any offline edits should queue and sync on `online`. Verify this explicitly on a real iPhone after 7+ days of disuse.

**Reorder the roadmap around impact and novelty:**
4. **Promote the condition/concentration tracker and the carnival minigames within NEXT-1; treat the dice feed as the lowest-priority item in that phase.** The dice feed is derivative (D&D Beyond already does it) and the minigames are your novel Chapter-1 pacing weapon — and Chapter 1 is exactly where your first sessions will live. Ship what makes session 1–3 sing.
5. **Ship the fey-contract signature (NEXT-2) sooner than planned** — it's one of only two genuinely novel features and is low-risk technically (signature_pad is mature). It's a signature "wow" moment with minimal backend.
6. **Keep the Reliquary audio (NEXT-3) but budget for the iOS tap-to-unlock UX** and don't cache large audio in the PWA (50MB cap). Frame the tap as part of the ceremony.

**Campaign-craft actions (do regardless of build):**
7. **Run a real Session Zero** and use the divination forge as its centerpiece; set an explicit, generous-but-clear phones-at-the-table policy that positions the app as an in-fiction artifact. Build inter-character connections.
8. **Add sidekick rules** for the 3-player party and use the sidekick as the diegetic home for a "tether" effect.
9. **Pre-load the DM's Book with the Witchlight callback tracker** (wishes, Kettlesteam/Cloppington clues, Chapter-4 payoffs) and a homebrew merchant table.

**Design guardrails:**
10. **Implement `prefers-reduced-motion` and an in-app motion toggle** across all ceremony animations before adding more of them.
11. **Use private Supabase channels with RLS** for whispers and dice, throttle minigame broadcasts to ≤10Hz batched, and write anything durable to Postgres rather than relying on broadcast (72-hour drop).
12. **Audit for dynamically constructed Tailwind class names** in the animation/ceremony code (v4 purges them silently).

## Caveats

- **Some sources are marketing or SEO content.** Tableward's description is self-published beta marketing; several "best app" roundups (ScreenRant, MakeUseOf) and some pricing/limits blogs are affiliate/SEO pages — I used them only to establish market focus and cross-checked hard numbers (Supabase/Vercel limits, SRD details) against primary docs where possible.
- **Prices and free-tier limits change frequently.** Supabase's 7-day pause (and 90-day restore window), Vercel's 100GB/non-commercial Hobby terms, and pollinations.ai's model lineup were current as of mid-2026 but should be re-verified before you rely on them; the Supabase key deprecation (end of 2026) is a moving target.
- **The novelty assessment is a "no prior art found" result, not a proof of absence.** The finger-drawn fey contract and live carnival minigames appear novel based on the searches run, but the TTRPG-app space is fragmented (many small itch.io/hobby projects); treat "novel" as "no significant competitor," not "patentably unique."
- **IP risk is assessed for a private, free, unlisted fan app.** If the app is ever made public, monetized, or listed in an app store, the analysis changes materially — the Fan Content Policy's "free means FREE" rule and the pollinations.ai commercial/model-license questions would both need a fresh look.
- **I could not independently verify the app's current code** — the technical recommendations assume the architecture as described in the directive (Vite/React SPA, dual LocalStore/SupabaseStore, Supabase RPC + realtime).
